The Eye - Sprint I - Animatronic Independent Study

Eyeball and Eyelid Mechanism

The eyeball and eyelid mechanisms of animatronics were what originally drew me into this independent study, with an original goal as just a personal project to design and build a "personality core" from the Portal series by Valve. Because of this, of the three mechanisms I was pursuing, I probably knew the most about the eye going into this process. I chose user Ikkalebob's Instructables Eye for its ease of making, with most of the parts being 3D printed, which allowed me to look closely at what could be improved and considerations to be made. Like the Instructable suggests, it took about a day to get set up, not including print times. This was more a reaffirmation of what I had already been reading about coming into the independent study. I kept the base model mostly the same, adding small additions like screws to hold the servos in place and a bolt for the universal joint to seat onto instead of the plastic peg in the original design.

Getting to look and work with it physically I noticed a few things I will be keeping in mind for the next sprint. First the pivoting center of the universal joint is aligned with the holes used to attach the push rods to the rim of the eyeball. This does not seem to be a coincidence although it is something I will be playing with when I take a deeper dive and design my own eye next sprint. Next, relative to the size of the eyeball, the servo horn that controls the yaw rotation is about the same width. Given that the connection point between the pushrod and eyeball has some play in it, having a more equal ration of eyeball width to servo horn with seems to help prevent binding and ensure the angle of the servo matches with the eyeball. Of course, that small amount of play also helps give the eye the lifelike, imperfect shaking and fluidity found in a normal human eye.

For being designed in a day it worked fairly well although the width between the eyeball’s back rim and the upper eyelid left little room for any push rod connection. At the same time though this also meant the eyelid hugged the eyeball closely, giving it a more realistic look. One thing I want to look into is potentially an internal gimbal to control the eye’s pitch and yaw rotation as opposed to pulling directly on the sides. Aesthetic wise I was surprised what a bit of watered down sharpie could do for the look of an eyeball. While my independent study is formally in the control mechanisms of animatronics, I would like to explore and make them look like something you would find on a finished animatronic too.
