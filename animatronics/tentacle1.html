The Tentacle - Sprint I - Animatronic Independent Study

Tentacle Mechanism

Okay so this ended up being much nicer than either of the two other “rough” mechanisms. Being far more hardware heavy and more dependent on different factors I also felt I learned the most from it. Further setting it apart from the eye and the hand was that I opted to go for the puppeteering route first, as opposed to controlled by an Arduino (partly due to wanting to see how it felt to make something like what was used in practical effects for movies (Little Shop of Horrors (Audrey II), Super Mario Bros (Yoshi), partly because I do not have beefy enough servos… yet).

After reading some smaller Instructables and watching a lot of videos, I ended up going with a two stage tentacle, with the lower half being able to be manipulated independently of the first. This is achieved through two sets of four (could be more) cables under tension. The controllers ensure that as one side undergoes tension, the other is slackened, allowing the tentacle to bend in a given direction. The independence of the two stages comes from housing the control cables for the upper half in springs that run up through the lower half.

The tensioner controllers came last and were based off Poor Man’s Animatronics blog. They were a lot of fun to design and make and something different than the two axes controllers that I saw in most images. The variable tensioning, while a pain, made it so I didn’t have to commit to putting a ferrule on the cable, especially since I was still learning and want to experiment more with the tensioning. Along the way I also added my own touches, from embedding lock nuts during the 3D printing process (see image 1 and 3 below) allowing it all to be 3D printed but still be stable, as well as heat pressed threaded inserts to prevent the cable from chewing through the 3D printed PLA disks (see image 2 in challenges). Some scrap pieces from my college also allowed for an adjustable angle to the tentacle, which helped with tensioning.

There were some problems with the tensioning controllers as opposed to the more traditional two axes ones. First because the cables weren’t routed into tracks, they could easily twine around parts of the controller or bunch up between the plates, causing binding (see image 4 in challenges). I accidently stumbled upon the solution to this while trying to combat the opposite problem, fearing there would not be enough space between the plates to allow for the range of control I wanted, I made it so that space could be increased or decreased by adding nuts onto the “threaded rods” coming off of the universal joint. Shortening this space seemed to help prevent this binding and as I vastly overestimated how much space I needed to get a good range of motion, didn’t feel as if I was losing range of motion.

All in all I enjoy how it came out, if still a little rough in the fluidity of motion. I could spend another several hours re-tensioning past my first few attempts but feel I have a good understanding of what this type of tentacle system entails.

Finally some small details to call out:
  • Having the core affixed properly is critical. Some binding I though was due to spacing the vertebrae too far apart was actually from the core riding up (see image 3 in challenges).
  • Brazing the end of cut steel cable is a great way to keep it from fraying. I learned this too a little too late for this sprint.

  • Basic Woodshop - Mounting plate was cut and drilled in the Weissman Foundry woodshop
